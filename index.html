About

Built for the
Front Line

Jalil Lewis grew up in Park Slope, Brooklyn — which means he understood people before he understood paperwork. He didn't come into healthcare through a traditional path. He came through the door most people overlook: front desk, back office, clinical floor — working every role that actually touches a patient.

At MedRite Urgent Care, Jalil moved through three distinct positions — lab technician, medical assistant, and front desk receptionist — building a complete, practical understanding of how healthcare facilities run. It was during his time there, working directly with patients, that Dr. Raven Drummond came in as a patient. She recognized something in him — a quality of presence and attentiveness that doesn't come from training. She invited him into dentistry.

At Lumia Dental, he was trained from the ground up by Annie Chung, the practice's head treatment coordinator. Annie taught him the standards, the systems, and the discipline that separates a great coordinator from a forgettable one. When Annie eventually resigned, Jalil didn't simply fill the vacancy — he formalized everything she'd modeled into written protocol, built training materials for incoming coordinators, and made the standard replicable.

What followed speaks for itself: over $597,000 in accepted treatment plans, a practice that grew from 7 to 14 operatories, a 28% reduction in no-shows, and a $30,000 pro bono case for a Providence House resident who had never in her life received comprehensive dental care — at no cost to her.

The Method

Treatment Planning
Approach

Case acceptance isn't a sales technique. It's what happens when a patient feels genuinely informed, respected, and cared for. Every step below is built around that belief.

01

Listen First

Before presenting anything, understand what the patient is actually worried about — cost, time, fear, a bad past experience. The treatment plan means nothing until you know what's standing between them and yes.

02

Translate, Don't Summarize

Clinical language creates distance. Jalil breaks down every diagnosis into plain terms — what it is, why it matters now, and what happens if it waits. Patients leave the consult actually understanding what they need, not just nodding along.

03

Present the Full Picture

Cost, insurance coverage, out-of-pocket breakdown, financing options — all of it, upfront and clearly. No surprises at checkout. When patients feel they have complete information, the decision becomes theirs to make freely. That's when acceptance rates climb.

04

Follow Through, Every Time

The consult is the beginning, not the end. Jalil's protocol includes structured follow-up — a personal message, a check-in, a reason to come back that isn't pressure. This is where the 28% no-show reduction happened. Patients who feel remembered show up.

Healthcare Equity in Practice

Dental care is among the most financially inaccessible forms of healthcare in the United States. Jalil has witnessed that reality firsthand — in patients who delay treatment for years, in families navigating insurance gaps, in people who have learned to simply live with pain. He has been committed, since the beginning of his career, to ensuring that financial barriers are not always the final answer.

Providence House — A $30,000 Pro Bono Case

Providence House is a Brooklyn-based organization with over 40 years of experience supporting women rebuilding their lives after incarceration, domestic violence, and housing instability. A case manager there contacted Lumia Dental on behalf of a resident who had never received comprehensive dental care. The treatment she needed — decades of deferred procedures finally addressed — exceeded $30,000.

Jalil coordinated every aspect of the case. Every appointment. Every specialist referral. Every check-in with a patient who had spent her life being told that this level of care simply wasn't available to her. The practice absorbed the cost entirely. She received complete, comprehensive dental care — every procedure, completed, at no cost to her.

He doesn't mention it for recognition. He mentions it because it crystallized, more clearly than anything else in his professional life, what treatment coordination is actually for. It is not purely a revenue function. At its core, it is the moment when a person decides whether they trust the healthcare system enough to take care of themselves. His job is to make that decision easier.

"That case is his north star. When he talks about why this work matters, he comes back to that woman — and what it meant to finally be able to say yes, when the answer had always been no."
